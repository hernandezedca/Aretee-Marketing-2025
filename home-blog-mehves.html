Our Relationship with Death Was More Intimate in the Middle Ages

In the Middle Ages, death was not hidden or postponed—it was visible, familiar, and unavoidable. Through sermons, art, and literature, medieval society developed one of the most radical reflections on equality ever imagined: the Dance of Death.

In the Middle Ages, a person’s place in life was decided from the very beginning—at birth. At the top stood kings and bishops, ruling the world as if by divine command. Just below them were lords who controlled land and people, alongside a growing bourgeois class. At the bottom were the peasants, bound to the soil, their bodies claimed by labor and their lives spent in deprivation. The walls between these worlds were thick: people might breathe the same air, but they could never occupy the same ground.

Social order was accepted as destiny. One was expected to die where one was born. Life was a privilege for those above, and a long act of penance for those below.

Medieval people thought of themselves as beings preparing for death while still alive—because life itself was a rehearsal for judgment.

For medieval people, death was not something to be pushed aside or postponed, as it often is today. Death was present—visible, familiar, constantly recalled. It appeared in church sermons, on gravestones, in manuscripts, on painted walls. True balance and justice were not expected in this world, but beyond death.

What we now call the Dance of Death (Danza de la Muerte) is not simply a grim image or a passing literary trend born after the Black Death. It is one of the most uncompromising statements of social equality in human history. In these narratives, death is not a catastrophe—it is the only space where justice, impossible on earth, can finally take shape.

The structure of Dance of Death texts is strikingly consistent. Death enters the scene and confronts the living one by one, forcing them into dialogue. Each figure speaks in their own voice, defined by rank, profession, and social role. Kings appear, as do monks; doctors step forward, followed by peasants.

Visual representations follow the same logic: figures are lined up in sequence, and Death approaches each from the same distance. Everyone is called by name and status, yet the outcome never changes. What they confront is not death itself, but the inevitability of being leveled.

Death Did Not Dance the Same Way Everywhere

Medieval Europe was far from uniform. Ideas changed shape as they moved across regions and social contexts. People believed in the same God and shared similar fears, but as urban life, political structures, daily experience, and even humor shifted, Death took on different roles. The Dances of Death reflect these differences.

Facing Death in Paris

One of the most influential Dance of Death representations appeared in early fifteenth-century Paris, painted on the walls of the Cimetière des Saints-Innocents. Against a backdrop of decaying bones, Death links clerics, emperors, and peasants into a single chain—binding all social orders together.

Although the original fresco has been lost, its influence survived through texts and printed editions. Most notably, in 1485, Guyot Marchant published La Danse Macabre in Paris. The term macabre signals not joy, but unease—death itself takes the stage.

Marchant reshaped the cemetery imagery into French verse, pairing moral warnings with a carefully ordered procession of figures. Readers do more than observe death’s march—they follow it, step by step, as it draws everyone into the same rhythm.

An excerpt from Guyot Marchant’s 1486 edition of Danse Macabre, featuring Death alongside a monk, a usurer, and a poor man.

The Totentanz Tradition

The German Totentanz shares the same core idea: the harsh equality of a common end. In Lübeck’s Marienkirche (1463), Death declares, “You must all dance to my tune.” No one escapes the rhythm.

Later works, including the Heidelberger Totentanz and Holbein’s sixteenth-century designs, make Death even more immediate. It no longer waits at the edge of life—it intrudes suddenly, without warning.

The Iberian Peninsula: One Death, Three Voices

Iberian Dances of Death rely primarily on written texts rather than monumental images. In Castile, La danza general de la muerte adopts a didactic, sermon-like tone. Death warns, interrogates, and exposes worldly ambition.

The Catalan Dança de la Mort (1497), shaped by Pere Miquel Carbonell, reflects notarial precision rather than emotional drama. Closely aligned with the French tradition, it carefully controls order and discourse.

The Sevillian version, printed in 1520, expands the cast to include artisans and urban professions. Here, Death exposes not only equality, but the fractures and decay of the city itself.

Ultimately, no social order built in this world survives the final scene.

Today, death has been removed from public life and confined to managed, private spaces. Medieval people met death face to face. We postpone it—through plans, insurance, and promises of “later.”

Yet the message of the Dance of Death remains. Titles, wealth, and reputations fall silent once the dance begins. What remains is not who you were, but how you lived.

About Mehveş

M. Mehveş Öçal is a literary scholar working on medieval Spanish literature. While much of her research engages with the Dance of Death tradition, her interests extend beyond death culture to allegory, social hierarchy, and the ways medieval texts reflect everyday life, culture and belief systems. She holds an MA in Spanish Language and Literature from Istanbul University and works in research, translation, and cultural writing.
